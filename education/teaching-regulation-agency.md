# Teaching Regulation Agency (TRA)

>[!WARNING]
>While efforts are made to not discuss such subjects in this entry, some content to discover references themes of abuse, including but not limited towards children.

The Teaching Regulation Agency (TRA) is an executive agency responsible for regulating the teaching profession in England on behalf of the Secretary of State. One core responsibility of the TRA is to investigate allegations of serious misconduct by teachers. When the TRA has concluded an investigation it can be referred to a professional conduct panel who will decide if that teacher is guilty of unacceptable professional conduct.

In a professional conduct panel, the outcomes are as follows:
- **Prohibition Order**: The person is prohibited from the teaching profession and cannot teach in schools, sixth form colleges, youth accomodations or children's homes in England for an indefinite period with or without a possibility of appeal after a period of time (minimum 2 years)
- **No order made**: The person is not prohibited from the teaching profession.

When a professional conduct panel outcome is complete, the outcome of the panel is published on GOV.UK, and can be read by anyone.

When a no order made decision is published, the teacher is still guilty of unacceptable professional conduct by having an allegation against them that is proven, just that it is not worthy of being prohibited of teaching. If a panel does not find any facts to be proven or that they do not amount to unacceptable professional conduct, then publishing of the decision is at the discretion of the teacher.

For more information on the procedure regarding the disciplinary procedure for teachers, see [Teacher misconduct: Disciplinary procedures for the teaching profession](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/63fc912d8fa8f527ff6b4271/Teacher_misconduct_Disciplinary_procedures_for_the_teaching_profession_May_2020.pdf)

## Discoverable information

- Teacher full name
- Teacher reference number
- Teacher date of birth
- Former employer

Some case information may provide further information about the teacher, including whereabouts, activites they were involved in, transcripts of communications, and much more.

## Additional comments

In very rare cases, prohibition order documents may withhold the identity of the teacher entirely. Some real examples of this that are known are:

- "[Teacher X](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/62cd949bd3bf7f2ffaa4aa60/Teacher_X_Professional_conduct_panel_outcome.pdf)": An entirely pseudonymized account of a teacher's sexual relationship with a student.
- "[Teacher ZZZ](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/6944505436f089d38be1f355/TRATMPO_Teacher_ZZZ_conduct_panel_outcome.pdf)": Conduct panel outcome of a male teacher who refused to make any response to allegations of sending malicious communications to staff on fake accounts and sending students home without valid reasoning.

Some prohibition orders have also been modified after release, a notable example is with [**REDACTED** and Arding](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/64b946f306f78d000d7425fc/SoS_decision_for_Jennifer_Arding.pdf) where the first teacher's name and any information relating to her have been entirely blacked out. Despite this, news articles exist naming the redacted person.

Some conduct panel outcomes have been removed from the GOV.UK site later. One I had observed had been a No Order made, but without knowing the circumstances to why it was removed, I will not link here. Researchers are open to contact me for the example in my possession.

One noteworthy conduct panel outcome, [Mr. Marshall](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/62fdf2678fa8f504c6db7072/_WEB_DECISION__Marshall__Thomas_-_SoS_Decision_-_with_warning.pdf) was the only one I could find with the cover page emblazoned with a warning for explicit language.
